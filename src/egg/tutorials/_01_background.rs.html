4# Concepts: e-graphs and equality saturation
5
6<style> em { display: inline-block } </style>
7
8An _e-graph_ is a data structure that powers the _equality saturation_
9optimization technique.
10
11Both e-graphs
12(Gregory Nelson's [PhD Thesis](https://courses.cs.washington.edu/courses/cse599f/06sp/papers/NelsonThesis.pdf), 1980)
13and equality saturation
14([Tate et. al., 2009](https://arxiv.org/abs/1012.1802))
15were invented before.
16`egg` aims to make them easy, extensible, and efficient.
17
18This tutorial will approach these concepts at a high level.
19More detail can be found in our [paper].
20
21## Why any of this?
22
23When building pretty much any tool relating to programming languages,
24  you'll typically be working with _terms_ from your language
25  (we will use the words _term_, _expression_, and _program_ interchangeably).
26You may be trying to:
27- Convert a term to a "better", equivalent term (optimization);
28- Build a term from scratch according to a specification (synthesis);
29- Or show that two terms are equivalent (verification).
30
31_Term rewriting_ is common technique for working with terms
32  toward any of these goals.
33You start with a term _t_ and a set of rewrites,
34  each of which take the form _l → r_.
35For each rewrite,
36you try to match the pattern _l_ against the term _t_,
37  generating a substitution _σ_ at some subterm,
38  and then you apply that substitution to the right-hand pattern _r_
39  and replace the matching subterm.
40
41For example, consider the term 42 × (7 + 7) and the rewrite _x + x → 2 × x_:
42- The left-hand side would match at the subterm 7 + 7,
43  generating the substitution _σ_ = {_x_: 7}.
44- Applying the substitution gives _r\[σ\] =_ 2 × 7.
45  (_r\[σ\]_ means apply the substitution _σ_ to _r_)
46- Replacing the matched subterm 7 + 7 with _r\[σ\] =_ 2 × 7 gives the result: 42 × (2 × 7).
47
48One issue with term rewriting
49  (and other programming language techniques that involve manipulating terms)
50  is the matter of choice.
51Rewriting is _destructive_,
52  meaning that once you do a rewrite, the initial term is gone.
53If you have many rewrites to choose from,
54  picking one is like choosing a fork in the road:
55  going back and choosing another way is expensive or impossible,
56  so you're committed to the path you've chosen.
57Furthermore, picking the right rewrite at the right time is very difficult in general;
58  sometimes, a choice that seems good locally may be wrong globally.
59
60As an example, a C optimizer would like to rewrite `a * 2` to the cheaper `a << 1`.
61But what about `(a * 2) / 2`?
62A greedy, local approach will take a "wrong turn" resulting in `(a << 1) / 2`,
63  which is better than the input expression
64  but hides the fact that we could have canceled the multiplication and division.
65
66There are some techniques to mitigate this
67  (backtracking and [value-numbering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value_numbering)),
68  but fundamentally, the only way around it is to take _all choices at the same time_.
69But rewriting is destructive,
70  so applying _n_ rewrites to a term results in _n_ terms,
71  and then applying _n_ rewrites to each of those _n_ terms yields _n_<sup>2</sup> more terms,
72  and so on.
73If you want to explore _m_ "layers" deep in this tree,
74  you'll have to store _n_<sup><i>m</i></sup> terms,
75  which is prohibitively expensive.
76
77E-graphs can solve this representation problem.
78When rewriting a bunch of terms,
79  the results are often structurally similar.
80E-graphs can store large sets of similar expressions very compactly.
81Using e-graphs,
82  you can apply many rewrites simultaneously without a
83  multiplicative growth in space.
84Equality saturation is a technique to do this kind of rewriting for program
85  optimization.
86
87## What's an e-graph?
88
89An _e-graph_ ([/'igraf/][sound]) is a data structure to maintain
90  an [equivalence relation]
91  (really a [congruence relation], see the [next section](#invariants-and-rebuilding))
92  over expressions.
93An e-graph is a set of equivalence classes (_e-classes_),
94each of which contains equivalent _e-nodes_.
95An e-node is an operator with children, but instead of
96children being other operators or values, the children are e-classes.
97In `egg`, these are represented by the [`EGraph`], [`EClass`], and
98[`Language`] (e-nodes) types.
99
100Even small e-graphs can represent a large number of expressions, exponential in
101the number of e-nodes.
102This compactness is what makes e-graphs a compelling data structure.
103We can define what it means to _represent_ (or _contain_) a term as follows:
104
105- An e-graph represents a term if any of its e-classes do.
106- An e-class represents a term if any of its e-nodes do.
107- An e-node `f(n1, n2, ...)` represents a term `f(t1, t2, ...)` if e-class `ni` represents term `ti`.
108
109Here are some e-graphs.
110We picture e-classes as dotted boxes surrounding the equivalent e-nodes.
111Note that edges go from e-nodes to e-classes (not to other e-nodes),
112  since e-nodes have e-classes as children.
113Don't worry about how they are made just yet, we'll get to rewriting soon.

122An e-graph can be queried for patterns through a procedure called _e-matching_
123  (matching up to equivalence),
124  which searches the e-graph for e-classes that represent terms that match a given pattern.
125`egg`'s e-matching
126  (inspired by
127  [De Moura and Bjørner](http://citeseerx.ist.psu.edu/viewdoc/download?doi=10.1.1.800.2292&rep=rep1&type=pdf#page=193))
128  is done by the [`Searcher`]  and [`Pattern`] APIs.
129There are two core operations that modify the e-graph:
130  [`add`] which adds e-nodes to the e-graph, and
131  [`union`] which merges two e-classes.
132
133These operations can be combined to do rewriting over the e-graph.
134Critically, this rewriting process is only additive,
135  meaning that the _e-graph never forgets_ old versions of terms.
136Additionally, rewrites add entire _classes_ of terms at a time.
137To perform a rewrite _l → r_ over an e-graph,
138  you first search _l_ in the e-graph, yielding pairs of (_c_, _σ_),
139  where _c_ is the matching e-class and _σ_ is the substitution.
140Then, you `add` the term _r\[σ\]_ to the e-graph
141  and `union` it with the matching e-class _c_.
142
143Let's put it all together with an example referring to the four e-graphs in the
144  image above.
145
1461. The initial e-graph represents the term _(a × 2) / 2_.
147   Since each e-class only has one e-node,
148     the e-graph is basically an abstract syntax tree
149    with sharing (the 2 is not duplicated).
1502. Applying the rewrite _x × 2 → x << 1_
151   has recorded the fact that _a × 2 = a << 1_
152     without forgetting about _a × 2_.
153   Note how the newly added _a << 1_ refers to the existing "_a_" e-node,
154   and the "<<" e-node has been unioned into the same e-class
155   as the equivalent "×" e-node where the pattern _x × 2_ matched.
1563. Applying rewrite _(x × y) / z → x × (y / z)_ realizes that division
157    associates with multiplication.
158   This rewrite is critical to discovering the cancellation of 2s that we are looking for,
159     and it still works despite the fact that we applied the "wrong" rewrite previously.
1604. Applying rewrites _x / x → 1_ and _x × 1 → x_ doesn't add any new e-nodes,
161     since all the e-nodes were already present in the e-graph.
162   The result only unions e-classes,
163     meaning that e-graph actually got _smaller_ from applying these rewrites,
164     even though it now represents more terms.
165   In fact, observe that the top-right "×" e-node's left child is _itself_;
166     this cycle means the e-class represents the _infinite_ (!) set of terms
167     _a_, _a × 1_, _a × 1 × 1_, and so on.

169## Invariants and Rebuilding
170
171An e-graph has two core operations that modify the e-graph:
172[`add`] which adds e-nodes to the e-graph, and
173[`union`] which merges two e-classes.
174These operations maintains two key (related) invariants:
175
1761. **Congruence**
177
178   An e-graph maintains not just an [equivalence relation] over
179   expressions, but a [congruence relation].
180   Congruence basically states that if _x_ is equivalent to _y_,
181     _f(x)_ must be equivalent to _f(y)_.
182   So as the user calls [`union`], many e-classes other than the given
183   two may need to merge to maintain congruence.
184
185   For example, suppose terms _a + x_ and _a + y_ are represented in
186   e-classes 1 and 2, respectively.
187   At some later point, _x_ and _y_ become
188   equivalent (perhaps the user called [`union`] on their containing
189   e-classes).
190   E-classes 1 and 2 must merge, because now the two "+"
191   operators have equivalent arguments, making them equivalent.
192
1932. **Uniqueness of e-nodes**
194
195   There do not exist two distinct e-nodes with the same operators and equivalent
196   children in the e-graph, either in the same e-class or different e-classes.
197   This is maintained in part by the hashconsing performed by [`add`],
198   and by deduplication performed by [`union`] and [`rebuild`].
199
200`egg` takes a delayed approach to maintaining these invariants.
201Specifically, the effects of calling [`union`] (or applying a rewrite,
202which calls [`union`]) may not be reflected immediately.
203To restore the e-graph invariants and make these effects visible, the
204user *must* call the [`rebuild`] method.
205
206`egg`'s choice here allows for a higher performance implementation.
207Maintaining the congruence relation complicates the core e-graph data
208structure and requires an expensive traversal through the e-graph on
209every [`union`].
210`egg` chooses to relax these invariants for better performance, only
211restoring the invariants on a call to [`rebuild`].
212The [paper] on `egg` goes into greater detail on why this design choice makes
213  things faster.
214See the [`rebuild`] documentation for more information on
215  what it does and when you have to call it.
216Note also that [`Runner`]s take care of this for you, calling
217  [`rebuild`] between rewrite iterations.
218
219## Equality Saturation
220
221
222Equality saturation
223  ([Tate et. al., 2009](https://arxiv.org/abs/1012.1802))
224  is a technique for program optimization.
225`egg` implements a variant of equality saturation in the [`Runner`] API that
226  looks like the following pseudocode:
227
228```ignore
229fn equality_saturation(expr: Expression, rewrites: Vec<Rewrite>) -> Expression {
230    let mut egraph = make_initial_egraph(expr);
231
232    while !egraph.is_saturated_or_timeout() {
233        let mut matches = vec![];
234
235        // read-only phase, invariants are preserved
236        for rw in rewrites {
237            for (subst, eclass) in egraph.search(rw.lhs) {
238                matches.push((rw, subst, eclass));
239            }
240        }
241
242        // write-only phase, temporarily break invariants
243        for (rw, subst, eclass) in matches {
244            eclass2 = egraph.add(rw.rhs.subst(subst));
245            egraph.union(eclass, eclass2);
246        }
247
248        // restore the invariants once per iteration
249        egraph.rebuild();
250    }
251
252    return egraph.extract_best();
253}
254```
255
256Most of this was covered above, but we need to define two new terms:
257
258- _Saturation_ occurs when an e-graph detects that rewrites no longer add new information.
259  Consider the commutative rewrite _x + y → y + x_.
260  After applying it once, the second time adds no new information
261    since the e-graph didn't forget about the initial _x + y_ terms.
262  If all the rewrites are in this state, we say the e-graph is _saturated_,
263    meaning that the e-graph encodes all possible equivalences derivable from
264    the given rewrites.
265- _Extraction_ is a procedure for picking a single represented term from an e-class
266  that is optimal according to some cost function.
267  `egg`'s [`Extractor`]s provide this functionality.
268
269Put together,
270  equality saturation explores all possible variants of a
271  program that can be derived from a set of rewrites,
272  and then it extracts the best one.
273This solves the problem of choice described above,
274  as equality saturation essentially applies every rewrite every iteration,
275  using the e-graph to avoid exponential explosion.
276
277It sounds a little too good to be true,
278  and while it definitely has its caveats,
279  `egg` addresses many of the shortcomings from this approach,
280  making it feasible to apply on real-world problems.
281We've already discussed how rebuilding makes `egg`'s e-graphs fast,
282  and later tutorials will discuss how [`Analysis`] makes this approach flexible
283  and able to handle more than just syntactic rewriting.
